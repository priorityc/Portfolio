Black Hole Event

Landing Page

Live Demo GitHub Repo Back to Projects

Project Overview

Role

Front-end Developer

Timeline

1 months

Tools

HTML, CSS, JavaScript, Animations

Responsibilities

UI engineering, Front-end development, responsive layout

Case Study

The London Museum of Science had just launched a thrilling new astronomy event and wanted a digital experience that felt as magical as the night sky itself. The museum needed a landing page that didn’t just inform people, but pulled them into the universe. Something immersive, atmospheric, and family‑friendly. A moment of wonder that would inspire visitors to subscribe to the event — and hopefully support the museum through optional donations. Their goal was to increase awareness, attract families, and encourage sign‑ups for the event.

Problem

Their existing website announcements weren’t capturing attention — visitors were scrolling past without feeling the excitement. Many museum landing pages rely on static banners or text‑heavy announcements, which fail to create emotional engagement. The museum wanted something more immersive — a visually striking, interactive experience that would spark curiosity and encourage visitors to explore further.

Process

Research & Insights

Before designing the landing page, I explored how museums and science institutions typically present special events online. Many rely on static banners, long text blocks, or generic hero images that fail to create emotional engagement. This helped me identify several key insights:

  • Users respond strongly to visual storytelling
  • Museum websites often feel informational, not inspirational
  • Interactive elements increase engagement and conversions
  • The event theme should shape the entire experience

Gallery

Design Decisions

I implemented dynamic data handling with the wellness API, optimized component rendering, and ensured keyboard navigation and screen reader support across all interactive elements. Instead of a static banner, I used motion to create an immediate sense of immersion. The swirling black hole animation acts as a visual anchor, pulling users into the experience and encouraging them to scroll. This supports the museum’s goal of capturing attention quickly. Astronomy visuals can be intense, so I balanced the animation with a clean, uncluttered layout. Based on research, parents want quick clarity. I placed the event title, date, and “Free Entry” information prominently above the fold. This ensures families immediately understand the value of the event.

Technical Challenges

Building the swirling black‑hole effect required balancing visual quality with performance. I experimented with CSS animations, gradients, and layered transforms to achieve a sense of depth without relying on heavy video files or WebGL. The challenge was creating motion that felt fluid while keeping the page lightweight.

Outcome & Lessons Learned

The final landing page successfully delivers an immersive, astronomy‑themed experience that captures users’ attention within the first few seconds. The dynamic black‑hole animation, combined with clear messaging and scroll‑triggered interactions, creates a sense of curiosity and excitement that aligns with the museum’s event. The design communicates key information quickly, making it easy for families to understand what the event is about and how to subscribe.

Service quote calculator

Project Overview

Role

Interaction designer Developer

Timeline

6 months

Tools

Papper prototype, Power point

Responsibilities

UI engineering

Case Study

A small construction business needed a fast, reliable way to generate service quotes while working on the move. Manual calculations were slow and inconsistent, reducing client trust. This project explores a simple, mobile‑friendly solution that supports quick, transparent quoting during site visits.

Problem

The business owner manages all operations alone and has little time for manual calculations. Slow quoting leads to delays, inconsistent pricing, and lost client trust. A faster, more accurate solution was needed.

Process

This project followed an iterative, user‑centred design approach: research → insights → prototype → feedback → refinement.

Research

  • Context exploration
  • Understanding user environment
  • Observing real workflow
  • Identifying pain points

Insights

  • Paint points exploration
  • Understanding user environment
  • Opportunities for improvement
  • What the user actually needs

Design Decisions

The design prioritises quick, simple actions because the business owner works on site...

  • Mobile-first flow: Designed for quick use during site visits.
  • Step-by-step structure: Mirrors how contractors naturally assess jobs.
  • Minimal input: Automatic measurements, simple selectors.
  • Clear hierarchy: Large headings and spacing for fast scanning.
  • Real-world terminology: Uses language contractors already use.
  • Review screen: Builds trust and reduces errors.

Technical Challenges

  • Translating an unstructured, experience‑based quoting process into a clear digital flow
  • Deciding which inputs were essential while keeping the UI simple and fast.
  • Keeping the flow consistent across different steps with varied content.

Outcome & Lessons Learned

The final prototype delivers a clear, mobile‑friendly quoting flow that supports fast, on‑site use. It simplifies the contractor’s process, reduces manual input, and provides a structured way to capture essential details. The project strengthened my ability to translate real‑world workflows into intuitive digital interactions and helped me refine my approach to designing simple, efficient mobile experiences.
